\chapter{\label{navier-details}Navier-Stokes data generation details}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/navier_spectral.png}
    \caption{\label{fig:navier-spectral} Fourier spectra of an example
    initial and final vorticity field from the generated Navier-Stokes
    dataset. The initial spectrum reflects the truncated low-frequency
    initialization with Gaussian weighting, while the final spectrum
    shows the redistribution of spectral energy over time.}
\end{figure}

This appendix provides additional details on the generation of the
self-produced Navier-Stokes dataset used in the uniform grid
experiments. The dataset is formulated in vorticity form on the unit
square and is generated numerically from the incompressible
Navier-Stokes equation \cite{batchelor1967fluid,chorin1993fluid}:

\begin{equation}
    \partial_t \omega + u \cdot \nabla \omega = \nu \Delta \omega + f,
\end{equation}

where $\omega$ denotes the vorticity field, $u$ the incompressible
velocity field, $\nu$ the viscosity, and $f$ an external forcing term.
The velocity field is not part of the stored dataset or the prediction
target. Instead, it is reconstructed from the vorticity during data
generation in order to evaluate the nonlinear advection term, using the
streamfunction relation \cite{batchelor1967fluid,chorin1993fluid}:

\begin{equation}
    -\Delta \psi = \omega, \qquad u = \nabla^\perp \psi.
\end{equation}

In all generated samples, the viscosity is kept fixed at
$\nu = 10^{-5}$. The forcing term is also shared across samples and is
given by:

\begin{equation}
    f(y,t) = 0.5 \left(1 + 0.5 \sin(0.5 t)\right)\sin(4\pi y),
\end{equation}

which is consistent with the Kolmogorov-flow literature, where the
forcing is commonly chosen as a single sinusoidal mode that varies only
in one spatial direction \cite{chandler2013kolmogorov,vela2024kolmogorov}.
In the present dataset, the spatial factor $\sin(4\pi y)$ serves as the
corresponding single-mode forcing on the unit square. The prefactor was
chosen so that the forcing remains smooth, bounded, and of moderate
magnitude throughout the simulation: specifically,
$0.5(1 + 0.5\sin(0.5 t)) \in [0.25, 0.75]$. The additional temporal
modulation is not intended to reproduce a specific benchmark forcing
law, but was introduced as a synthetic extension to generate trajectories
with mild time-dependent variation in the energy input while avoiding
abrupt or high-frequency changes.

The dataset contains 1000 samples at a resolution of $256 \times 256$.
For each sample, an initial vorticity field is generated in Fourier
space. Fourier coefficients are assigned for indices
$(k_x,k_y) = (i,j)$ with $i,j \in \{1,\dots,k_{\max}-1\}$ and
$k_{\max}=16$. These coefficients are random complex numbers whose
amplitudes are modulated by a Gaussian envelope of the form
$\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}\frac{i^2+j^2}{(k_{\max}/2)^2}\right)$, which
suppresses higher frequencies and yields smooth initial conditions. The
coefficients in the $-k_x,-k_y$ quadrant are then set by conjugate
symmetry so that the inverse Fourier transform produces a real-valued
field. No coefficients are assigned in the mixed-sign quadrants. The
initial vorticity field is finally obtained by applying an inverse
Fourier transform, followed by normalization such that the maximum absolute value of the vorticity is 1.

Each initial condition is then evolved forward in time using a
fourth-order Runge--Kutta (RK4) integration scheme to obtain the
corresponding later vorticity field stored in the dataset.


This initialization procedure produces smooth but diverse initial
conditions while concentrating most spectral energy in the low-frequency
region. Figure~\ref{fig:navier-spectral} illustrates this by showing the
Fourier spectra of an example initial state and its corresponding final
state. The figure highlights both the initially active modes and the
effect of the Gaussian envelope, as well as the redistribution of
spectral energy under the Navier-Stokes dynamics.


\chapter{\label{appendix:htgr-input-engineering}HTGR input engineering details}

This appendix provides additional details on the input engineering used
for the HTGR column temperature dataset, as introduced in
Chapter~\ref{cha:method}. In this dataset, the model input consists only
of the axial level $z$ and the boundary temperature $T$. Since this input
is low-dimensional, several alternative representations were tested to
determine whether a more expressive encoding of the same information
would improve predictive performance.

Table~\ref{tab:htgr-input-engineering} shows a representative subset of
the tested input representations. The raw representation $(z,T)$ already
performs well, with a test relative RMSE of $9.10 \times 10^{-3}$.
Normalizing the same two variables does not improve performance in this
case, and instead gives a slightly higher test relative RMSE of
$9.51 \times 10^{-3}$. This suggests that normalization alone is not
necessarily beneficial for this dataset, and that the model may benefit
from access to the original numerical scale of the axial position and
boundary temperature.

The best-performing representation among the tested configurations is
the normalized polynomial encoding $(z,T,z^2,T^2,zT)$, which reduces the
test relative RMSE to $9.46 \times 10^{-4}$. The added polynomial terms
are not intended to represent new physical variables, and in particular
$z^2$ and $T^2$ should not be interpreted as directly meaningful
physical quantities for this problem. Instead, they provide the model
with a richer representation of the simple scalar inputs, making certain
nonlinear dependencies easier to learn. The fact that the basic
normalized representation performs worse than the raw input, while the
normalized polynomial representation performs best, also illustrates that
the effect of feature engineering choices is not always straightforward.

A second engineered representation was tested using features with a
clearer connection to the geometry of the problem:
$(z,T,\sin(\pi(z-z_{\min})/L_z),T\sin(\pi(z-z_{\min})/L_z),
\cos(\pi(z-z_{\min})/L_z))$. The sine terms vanish at the axial
boundaries, while the cosine term equals one at the boundaries, giving
the model access to features that encode boundary-aware structure in the
$z$ direction. This representation achieves a test relative RMSE of
$2.58 \times 10^{-3}$, substantially improving over the raw
representation but not matching the normalized polynomial model. It also
converged in substantially less time in this experiment. This result
suggests that physically motivated feature choices can be effective, but
also that physical interpretability alone does not guarantee the best
empirical performance.

Overall, these results show that the choice of input representation has
a significant effect on the HTGR prediction task. The normalized
polynomial representation performs best among the configurations shown
here, while the boundary-aware trigonometric representation provides a
useful comparison: it is physically easier to motivate and improves over
the raw input, but does not reach the same accuracy as the best
polynomial encoding.
