\chapter{\label{navier-details}Navier-Stokes data generation details}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/navier_spectral.png}
    \caption{\label{fig:navier-spectral} Fourier spectra of an example
    initial and final vorticity field from the generated Navier-Stokes
    dataset. The initial spectrum reflects the truncated low-frequency
    initialization with Gaussian weighting, while the final spectrum
    shows the redistribution of spectral energy over time.}
\end{figure}

This appendix provides additional details on the generation of the
self-produced Navier-Stokes dataset used in the uniform grid
experiments. The dataset is formulated in vorticity form on the unit
square and is generated numerically from the incompressible
Navier-Stokes equation \cite{batchelor1967fluid,chorin1993fluid}:

\begin{equation}
    \partial_t \omega + u \cdot \nabla \omega = \nu \Delta \omega + f,
\end{equation}

where $\omega$ denotes the vorticity field, $u$ the incompressible
velocity field, $\nu$ the viscosity, and $f$ an external forcing term.
The velocity field is not part of the stored dataset or the prediction
target. Instead, it is reconstructed from the vorticity during data
generation in order to evaluate the nonlinear advection term, using the
streamfunction relation \cite{batchelor1967fluid,chorin1993fluid}:

\begin{equation}
    -\Delta \psi = \omega, \qquad u = \nabla^\perp \psi.
\end{equation}

In all generated samples, the viscosity is kept fixed at
$\nu = 10^{-5}$. The forcing term is also shared across samples and is
given by:

\begin{equation}
    f(y,t) = 0.5 \left(1 + 0.5 \sin(0.5 t)\right)\sin(4\pi y),
\end{equation}

which is consistent with the Kolmogorov-flow literature, where the
forcing is commonly chosen as a single sinusoidal mode that varies only
in one spatial direction \cite{chandler2013kolmogorov,vela2024kolmogorov}.
In the present dataset, the spatial factor $\sin(4\pi y)$ serves as the
corresponding single-mode forcing on the unit square. The prefactor was
chosen so that the forcing remains smooth, bounded, and of moderate
magnitude throughout the simulation: specifically,
$0.5(1 + 0.5\sin(0.5 t)) \in [0.25, 0.75]$. The additional temporal
modulation is not intended to reproduce a specific benchmark forcing
law, but was introduced as a synthetic extension to generate trajectories
with mild time-dependent variation in the energy input while avoiding
abrupt or high-frequency changes.

The dataset contains 1000 samples at a resolution of $256 \times 256$.
For each sample, an initial vorticity field is generated in Fourier
space. Fourier coefficients are assigned for indices
$(k_x,k_y) = (i,j)$ with $i,j \in \{1,\dots,k_{\max}-1\}$ and
$k_{\max}=16$. These coefficients are random complex numbers whose
amplitudes are modulated by a Gaussian envelope of the form
$\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}\frac{i^2+j^2}{(k_{\max}/2)^2}\right)$, which
suppresses higher frequencies and yields smooth initial conditions. The
coefficients in the $-k_x,-k_y$ quadrant are then set by conjugate
symmetry so that the inverse Fourier transform produces a real-valued
field. No coefficients are assigned in the mixed-sign quadrants. The
initial vorticity field is finally obtained by applying an inverse
Fourier transform, followed by normalization such that the maximum absolute value of the vorticity is 1.

Each initial condition is then evolved forward in time using a
fourth-order Runge--Kutta (RK4) integration scheme to obtain the
corresponding later vorticity field stored in the dataset.


This initialization procedure produces smooth but diverse initial
conditions while concentrating most spectral energy in the low-frequency
region. Figure~\ref{fig:navier-spectral} illustrates this by showing the
Fourier spectra of an example initial state and its corresponding final
state. The figure highlights both the initially active modes and the
effect of the Gaussian envelope, as well as the redistribution of
spectral energy under the Navier-Stokes dynamics.


\chapter{\label{appendix:htgr-input-engineering}HTGR input engineering details}

This appendix provides additional details on the input engineering used
for the HTGR column temperature dataset, as introduced in
Chapter~\ref{cha:method}. In this dataset, the model input consists only
of the axial level $z$ and the boundary temperature $T$. Since this input
is low-dimensional, several alternative representations were tested to
determine whether a more expressive encoding of the same information
would improve predictive performance.

Table~\ref{tab:htgr-input-engineering} shows a representative subset of
the tested input representations. The raw representation $(z,T)$ already
performs reasonably well, with a test relative RMSE of
$3.75 \times 10^{-3}$. Replacing these raw variables with min--max
normalized variables,
\[
    \hat z = \frac{z-z_{\min}}{z_{\max}-z_{\min}}, \qquad
    \hat T = \frac{T-T_{\min}}{T_{\max}-T_{\min}},
\]
scales both inputs to the range $[0,1]$. This improves the test relative
RMSE to $2.57 \times 10^{-3}$ and reduces the test MSE by approximately
a factor of two. Thus, for this dataset, giving the model inputs on a
common bounded scale is beneficial.

The next representation augments the min--max normalized variables with
quadratic and interaction terms, giving
$(\hat z,\hat T,\hat z^2,\hat T^2,\hat z\hat T)$. This further reduces
the test relative RMSE to $2.44 \times 10^{-3}$, although the improvement
over the basic min--max normalized representation is modest. This is
consistent with the fact that $\hat z^2$ and $\hat T^2$ are not physically meaningful variables for this problem. Instead, they provide a simple
polynomial expansion of the two scalar inputs, making some nonlinear
dependencies easier for the model to represent.

Finally, a trigonometric representation was tested using the min--max
normalized variables together with boundary-aware functions of the axial
coordinate:
$(\hat z,\hat T,\sin(\pi(z-z_{\min})/L_z),
 \cos(\pi(z-z_{\min})/L_z),\hat z\hat T)$, where
$L_z=z_{\max}-z_{\min}$. This choice is more directly motivated by the
structure of the data. In particular, the sine term vanishes at the
axial boundaries, which reflects the observed trend that the peak
temperature varies smoothly with $z$. The cosine term provides a complementary axial feature, and
the interaction term $\hat z\hat T$ gives the model direct access to
coupled dependence on axial position and boundary temperature.

The trigonometric representation gives the best performance among the
tested configurations, with a test relative RMSE of
$2.07 \times 10^{-3}$. This shows that using physical understanding
of the dataset in the input representation can improve the Geo-FNO
prediction accuracy. The trigonometric representation
was therefore used for the HTGR results reported in the main text.

\chapter{Geo-FNO Bug Fix}
\label{cha:geo-fno-bug-fix}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/navier_spectral.png}
    \caption{\label{fig:cylinder-flow-seam} Representative Geo-FNO prediction on the cylinder flow dataset before and after the bug fix. From left to right, this plot shows the ground truth, the original prediction with the seam artifact, the unpatched model trained without the learned coordinate mapping $\phi^{-1}$, and the corrected prediction after fixing the Fourier mode reconstruction.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/navier_spectral.png}
    \caption{\label{fig:iphi-before-after} Learned coordinate mapping $\phi^{-1}$ before and after the bug fix. The left panel shows the mapping learned by the original implementation, which exhibits a vertical split of the domain. The right panel shows the mapping learned after correcting the Fourier mode reconstruction, which no longer exhibits this artificial split.}
\end{figure}

During testing of the Geo-FNO, a persistent vertical seam-like artifact appeared across all tested datasets. The artifact was particularly visible in the cylinder flow dataset, where predictions showed a clear discontinuity along the center of the domain. An example is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cylinder-flow-seam}, where the predicted solution exhibits a sharp artificial transition that is not present in the ground truth.

Inspection of the learned coordinate transformation $\phi^{-1}$ revealed that it consistently split the domain along the same vertical line, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:iphi-before-after}. At first glance, this suggested that the learned coordinate mapping itself might be responsible for the artifact. However, training the model without $\phi^{-1}$ caused the seam to become substantially more pronounced, while the prediction quality away from the seam remained largely unchanged and the overall loss increased only modestly. This indicates that the learned behavior of $\phi^{-1}$ was not the source of the artifact, but rather an adaptation to it. By reducing the density of sampled points near the corrupted region, the mapping partially mitigated the visible effect of the reconstruction error without removing its underlying cause.

The source of the artifact was found to be an error in the reconstruction of discarded Fourier modes during the inverse transform. As in the standard FNO, geo-FNO retains only a finite number of Fourier modes in each direction. For a real-valued field, the Fourier coefficients satisfy the Hermitian symmetry condition
\begin{equation}
U(-k_x, -k_y) = \overline{U(k_x, k_y)},
\label{eq:hermitian-symmetry}
\end{equation}
where the overline denotes complex conjugation. This symmetry implies that only half of the Fourier spectrum needs to be stored explicitly, since the remaining coefficients can be reconstructed from their conjugate counterparts. In principle, any conjugate half of the spectrum could be discarded; in the Geo-FNO implementation, the modes with negative $k_y$ are omitted for memory efficiency and reconstructed during the inverse transform using Equation~\ref{eq:hermitian-symmetry}.

The implementation keeps only the modes with positive $k_y$, while separate learned complex weights are applied to the $(+k_x,+k_y)$ and $(-k_x,+k_y)$ branches. During reconstruction of the physical field, the discarded negative $k_y$ modes are recovered using Equation~\ref{eq:hermitian-symmetry}. The seam artifact was caused by an indexing error in this reconstruction step.

The original implementation reconstructed the discarded modes using a simple reversal of the stored mode ordering. For the $k_y$ direction, this is correct. However, for the $k_x$ direction, the Fourier modes are stored in the order
\begin{equation}
[0, 1, \dots, N-1, -N, -(N-1), \dots, -1].
\end{equation}

Two modes in this ordering are self-conjugate: the zero mode $k_x = 0$ and the Nyquist edge mode $k_x = -N$. Applying a direct reversal produces
\begin{equation}
[-1, -2, \dots, -N, N-1, N-2, \dots, 0],
\end{equation}
which incorrectly pairs the modes during reconstruction.

As a result, the implemented reconstruction effectively enforced
\begin{equation}
U(k_x, k_y)
=
\overline{U\bigl(-(k_x + 1), -k_y\bigr)},
\qquad k_x > 0,
\end{equation}
instead of the correct Hermitian symmetry relation in Equation~\ref{eq:hermitian-symmetry}. This mismatch was strongest for the lowest $k_x$ modes adjacent to the self-conjugate modes, causing corruption of the low-frequency components of the reconstructed field. Since these low modes dominate the global structure of the solution, the error appeared visually as a strong vertical seam through the center of the domain.

Correcting the reconstruction so that the discarded modes were matched to their proper conjugate partners removed the artifact entirely. For the cylinder flow dataset, this reduced the mean squared error from approximately $5.5 \times 10^{-3}$ to $4.7 \times 10^{-3}$, corresponding to a reduction of approximately 15\%. The visual seam disappeared completely, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cylinder-flow-seam}, while the learned $\phi^{-1}$ mapping no longer exhibited the artificial domain split shown in Figure~\ref{fig:iphi-before-after}.

The issue was reported to the authors of the original Geo-FNO implementation, and the proposed correction was subsequently incorporated into the official repository.
