The four models outlined in Section~\ref{uniform-experiments} were
evaluated on the Navier-Stokes dataset. Figure~\ref{fig:loss_vs_resolution_navier_stokes}
shows the MSE loss as a function of evaluation resolution for each
model, with the training resolution indicated by the vertical dashed
line. Table~\ref{tab:d-values-navier-stokes} reports the discretization
robustness score $D$, which measures how much the loss changes across
resolutions relative to the loss at the training resolution.

Among the FNO models, FNO\_k16 achieves the lowest loss at the training
resolution, but also has the largest value of $D$. Its performance
therefore changes the most under resolution transfer. This contrast is
especially pronounced at the lowest tested resolution, where the loss of
FNO\_k16 increases by more than two orders of magnitude compared with
its loss at the training resolution. FNO\_k8 has a higher loss at all tested resolution including the training resolution, but a substantially lower value of $D$, indicating
a more consistent loss across the evaluated resolutions. For both FNO
variants, the loss increases more rapidly when evaluating below the
training resolution than when evaluating above it.

The two CNO variants show much flatter loss curves than FNO\_k16 and
have nearly identical values of $D$. Their training-resolution losses are
also comparable. The standard CNO performs slightly better at the
training resolution, at lower resolutions, and at $N = 208$. At the
highest evaluated resolutions, CNO\_no\_UND gives slightly lower losses
than CNO\_standard. Overall, the Navier-Stokes results show a trade-off:
FNO\_k16 gives the best performance at the training resolution, while
FNO\_k8 and the CNO variants show more consistent behavior across
resolutions.

To test whether the trends observed on the Navier-Stokes dataset also
appear on a qualitatively different PDE benchmark, the four models were
evaluated on the Darcy flow dataset. The Darcy flow dataset was chosen as the solution space is smooth, making it a good point of comparison to the Navier-Stokes dataset. Figure~\ref{fig:loss_vs_resolution_darcy_flow}
shows the MSE loss as a function of evaluation resolution for each
model, with the training resolution indicated by the vertical dashed
line. Table~\ref{tab:d-values-darcy-flow} reports the corresponding
discretization robustness score $D$.

Compared with the Navier-Stokes experiment, the four models are closer
in overall performance on the Darcy flow dataset. At the training
resolution, CNO\_standard achieves the lowest loss, followed by
FNO\_k16, CNO\_no\_UND, and FNO\_k8. The two FNO variants show a similar
pattern to that observed for Navier-Stokes; the loss is lowest near the
training resolution and increases when the model is evaluated at other
resolutions. This increase is stronger below the training resolution
than above it. Notably, FNO\_k8 outperforms FNO\_k16 at resolutions below $N = 88$. This indicates that
retaining more Fourier modes does not consistently improve performance
under transfer to coarser grids.

The CNO variants show much flatter loss curves across the evaluated
resolutions, with $D$ values of order \(10^{-2}\). At lower resolutions,
both CNO models maintain an almost constant loss, while at higher
resolutions both losses increase slowly. In contrast to the
Navier-Stokes experiment, CNO\_standard outperforms CNO\_no\_UND at all
evaluated resolutions. The loss of CNO\_no\_UND also increases more
quickly than that of CNO\_standard when evaluating above the training
resolution. Overall, the Darcy flow results show a clearer separation
between the FNO and CNO variants in terms of discretization robustness;
the FNO models are more sensitive to resolution transfer, while the CNO
models remain nearly constant across the tested range.


\section{Discussion}

\subsection{FNO}

The FNO variants show the weakest discretization robustness of the models tested on both datasets. This is most clearly visible for FNO\_k16, which achieves the lowest loss at the training resolution on Navier--Stokes, but also the largest discretization robustness score $D$ by a large margin. This indicates that strong in-distribution performance does not imply robustness under resolution transfer.

A central reason for this behavior is that the meaning of each retained Fourier mode depends on the spatial resolution of the input grid. During training, the FNO is only exposed to a single discretization, and therefore implicitly learns an association between a mode's index and its physical meaning. For a fixed physical domain, a given Fourier mode index corresponds to the same nominal wavelength across resolutions. However, changing the resolution changes the highest resolvable frequency and therefore the distribution of spectral energy across the retained modes. At lower resolutions, unresolved high-frequency content may alias into the retained coefficients, while at higher resolutions that same information is represented explicitly in additional modes. As a result, the same retained mode coefficient may contain very different information depending on the discretization. When the resolution changes, this correspondence no longer holds; the same mode index no longer represents the same physical structure, and the learned spectral weights are applied to coefficients with different physical meaning than those seen during training.

This effect becomes particularly severe when evaluating below the training resolution. At lower resolutions, fewer frequencies are resolvable and some physically important modes are no longer represented correctly. High-frequency content folds into the retained modes through aliasing, and the spectral representation itself becomes corrupted. The model is therefore not only missing information, but is given misleading low-frequency content that was not present during training. In contrast, when evaluating above the training resolution, the model simply encounters additional high-frequency information that it was not trained to use. This is suboptimal, but less destructive than the irreversible information loss that occurs at coarse resolutions. This explains why the loss increases more rapidly below the training resolution than above it for both FNO variants.

The comparison between FNO\_k8 and FNO\_k16 highlights an important distinction between absolute accuracy and discretization robustness. Retaining more Fourier modes improves the expressive power of the model, which is why FNO\_k16 achieves lower loss than FNO\_k8 at the training resolution and, for the Navier--Stokes dataset, across all tested resolutions. The additional retained modes contain physically relevant information that improves prediction quality even when resolution transfer introduces mismatch.

However, these same modes are also the most sensitive to changes in discretization. Since higher-frequency coefficients are more strongly affected by aliasing, under-resolution, and changes in spectral energy distribution, relying on them makes the model less robust when evaluated away from the training grid. This is reflected in the much larger value of $D$ for FNO\_k16. FNO\_k8 is therefore not better in terms of predictive accuracy, but simply less sensitive because it relies on a more aggressively truncated and therefore more stable spectral representation.

This behavior is particularly clear on the Darcy flow dataset, where FNO\_k8 outperforms FNO\_k16 at sufficiently low resolutions. Darcy flow is a smooth elliptic problem with a strongly smoothing solution operator, meaning that the high-frequency content of the solution is relatively weak. The additional modes retained by FNO\_k16 therefore contain limited useful physical information and are more sensitive to discretization errors, poorly resolved modes, and interpolation noise. At coarse resolutions, relying on these modes becomes detrimental, while the more aggressively truncated FNO\_k8 remains more stable. At resolutions near or above training, where these modes are better resolved, the additional modes of FNO\_k16 again becomes advantageous.

For the Navier--Stokes dataset, the strong performance of FNO\_k16 at the training resolution is also partly artificial. As outlined in appendix \ref{navier-details}, the dataset is generated spectrally using 16 retained Fourier modes in each direction, meaning that an FNO retaining exactly 16 modes is naturally well matched to the structure of the data. This makes FNO\_k16 particularly well suited to the benchmark and gives it an advantage that may not transfer to more general datasets where the true solution is not already bandlimited to the retained modes.

\subsection{CNO}

In contrast to the FNO, the CNO variants show substantially stronger discretization robustness on both datasets. Their loss curves remain much flatter across evaluation resolutions, and the corresponding values of $D$ are significantly lower, particularly on the Darcy flow dataset where both CNO models remain nearly constant across much of the tested range.

A central reason for this behavior is the initial resampling step onto a fixed internal grid. Regardless of the input resolution, the CNO first interpolates the input field onto the same internal discretization before the main operator learning begins. This removes the resolution-dependent change in representation that causes much of the instability in the FNO. The convolutional blocks and nonlinear operations therefore always act on the same effective resolution, meaning that the model learns a more consistent operator across discretizations.

This behavior is particularly clear for the Darcy flow dataset. Darcy flow is governed by a smooth elliptic operator, and the solution space is strongly dominated by low-frequency structure. As a result, even relatively coarse evaluation grids still preserve the physically relevant information needed for prediction. Since the CNO always projects the input onto the same internal representation, evaluating at lower resolutions introduces only a small loss of information, and the model remains almost unchanged across the tested range.

At higher evaluation resolutions, the loss of the CNO increases gradually for both datasets. This is expected from the explicit bandlimited assumption built into the architecture. Because the model always resamples onto the same internal grid, frequencies beyond that effective bandlimit are removed before learning begins. Higher-resolution inputs therefore contain physically meaningful fine-scale information that the model deliberately discards. As the evaluation resolution increases, the difference between the bandlimited representation seen during training and the higher-bandwidth true field becomes larger, leading to a gradual increase in loss. Over the tested range, this increase is approximately linear.

The difference between CNO\_standard and CNO\_no\_UND is most visible at high resolutions, particularly for the Navier--Stokes dataset where CNO\_no\_UND slightly outperforms the standard CNO. This behavior can be understood by considering the role of the upsample--nonlinearity--downsample (UND) block described in Section~\ref{sec:cno}.

The purpose of the UND block is to prevent nonlinear activations from generating unresolved high-frequency content that aliases back into the retained modes. By temporarily upsampling before the activation and applying low-pass filtering during the subsequent downsampling step, the CNO preserves a more consistent bandlimited representation and improves continuum consistency.

However, the low-pass filter used during downsampling is only an approximation of the ideal sinc projection. In practice, it acts as a smoothing operation rather than a perfectly sharp spectral cutoff. While this successfully removes unresolved frequencies, it also attenuates the highest retained modes and slightly reduces access to the finest physically meaningful structures available to the model.

For Darcy flow, this has little practical consequence because the solution operator is strongly smoothing and the highest frequencies contain relatively little useful information. The standard CNO therefore performs better across all tested resolutions.

For the much more nonlinear Navier--Stokes problem, however, the highest retained modes are significantly more important. The attenuation imposed by the approximate low-pass filter effectively enforces an even stricter bandlimit on the learned representation. As evaluation resolution increases, the true solution contains progressively more physically meaningful high-frequency structure, and the loss grows according to how quickly the bandlimited training representation diverges from these higher-bandwidth fields. Because CNO\_standard is effectively trained with a stronger bandlimit than CNO\_no\_UND, this divergence occurs faster, which explains why CNO\_no\_UND slightly outperforms the standard CNO at the highest tested resolutions despite being theoretically less representation-equivalent.

This does not imply that the UND block should be removed in general. Across most settings, including all Darcy flow experiments and lower-resolution Navier--Stokes evaluations, the standard CNO performs as well or better and remains the more theoretically consistent architecture. Rather, these results suggest that the practical approximation of the ideal low-pass projection can become a limiting factor for highly nonlinear problems with significant high-frequency content.

\subsection{Improving the FNO}

To identify which parts of the CNO architecture are primarily responsible for its stronger discretization robustness, an ablation study was performed on the FNO. The two features hypothesized to be most important were resampling onto a fixed internal grid and the UND block. Rather than introducing both modifications at once, each was tested individually as well as in combination.

Four models were therefore compared: the standard FNO, FNO\_UND, which adds only the UND block, FNO\_resample, which adds only the fixed-grid resampling step, and FNO\_resample\_UND, which includes both modifications. All models retained 16 Fourier modes in each direction and were trained and evaluated on the Navier--Stokes dataset.

The results are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:improving-fno}, with the corresponding discretization robustness scores reported in Table~\ref{tab:d-values-navier-stokes-improved-fno}. One observation is that the UND block alone produces only a negligible improvement. The values of $D$ for FNO and FNO\_UND are nearly identical, indicating that while aliasing from nonlinear activations contributes to discretization mismatch, it is not the dominant failure mechanism responsible for the poor robustness of the standard FNO.

In contrast, introducing resampling onto a fixed internal grid produces a dramatic improvement. Both FNO\_resample and FNO\_resample\_UND reduce the discretization robustness score by nearly two orders of magnitude compared to the standard FNO. This strongly supports the interpretation developed in the previous sections: the primary source of instability in the FNO is not the nonlinearity itself, but the dependence of the retained Fourier coefficients on the input discretization.

Without resampling, the model learns on a fixed set of retained Fourier modes whose coefficients change meaning when the input resolution changes. Although the nominal wavelength associated with a mode index remains the same, the highest resolvable frequency changes with resolution, which alters the spectral energy distribution and introduces aliasing errors at coarse resolutions. As a result, the same retained coefficient may contain very different information depending on the discretization, causing the learned spectral weights to become inconsistent under resolution transfer.

Resampling resolves this problem by enforcing a fixed internal representation before the Fourier layers are applied. Regardless of the input resolution, the model always operates on the same effective discretization and therefore sees the same bandlimit and the same spectral structure during both training and inference. This restores the consistency between retained mode coefficients and their physical meaning, making the learned operator substantially more robust across resolutions.

The remaining small improvement from adding the UND block on top of resampling is consistent with the role of nonlinear aliasing described earlier. Once the dominant representation mismatch is removed by resampling, controlling the frequencies generated by nonlinear activations provides a small additional benefit, but does not fundamentally change the behavior of the model.

It is important to remember that the Navier--Stokes benchmark used here favors the FNO somewhat artificially. The initial conditions are generated spectrally using 16 retained Fourier modes in each direction, meaning that an FNO retaining exactly 16 modes is naturally well matched to the structure of the data. As a result, all tested resolutions are sufficiently fine to capture the full signal, and the resampled FNO does not show the gradual high-resolution loss increase observed for the CNO. For more general datasets where the true solution is not already bandlimited to the retained modes, a gradual increase in loss at higher resolutions would still be expected, governed by how quickly the fixed training representation diverges from higher-bandwidth fields as was seen for the CNO.

Overall, these results show that the dominant source of poor discretization robustness in the FNO is the resolution dependence of its spectral representation. By introducing the fixed internal resampling strategy used in the CNO, the FNO becomes dramatically more stable under resolution transfer, while the UND block provides a smaller secondary improvement.
