The four models outlined in Section~\ref{uniform-experiments} were
evaluated on the Navier-Stokes dataset. Figure~\ref{fig:loss_vs_resolution_navier_stokes}
shows the MSE loss as a function of evaluation resolution for each
model, with the training resolution indicated by the vertical dashed
line. Table~\ref{tab:d-values-navier-stokes} reports the discretization
robustness score $D$, which measures how much the loss changes across
resolutions relative to the loss at the training resolution.

Among the FNO models, FNO\_k16 achieves the lowest loss at the training
resolution, but also has the largest value of $D$. Its performance
therefore changes the most under resolution transfer. This contrast is
especially pronounced at the lowest tested resolution, where the loss of
FNO\_k16 increases by more than two orders of magnitude compared with
its loss at the training resolution. FNO\_k8 has a higher loss at all tested resolution including the training resolution, but a substantially lower value of $D$, indicating
a more consistent loss across the evaluated resolutions. For both FNO
variants, the loss increases more rapidly when evaluating below the
training resolution than when evaluating above it.

The two CNO variants show much flatter loss curves than FNO\_k16 and
have nearly identical values of $D$. Their training-resolution losses are
also comparable. The standard CNO performs slightly better at the
training resolution, at lower resolutions, and at $N = 208$. At the
highest evaluated resolutions, CNO\_no\_UND gives slightly lower losses
than CNO\_standard. Overall, the Navier-Stokes results show a trade-off:
FNO\_k16 gives the best performance at the training resolution, while
FNO\_k8 and the CNO variants show more consistent behavior across
resolutions.

To test whether the trends observed on the Navier-Stokes dataset also
appear on a qualitatively different PDE benchmark, the four models were
evaluated on the Darcy flow dataset. The Darcy flow dataset was chosen as the solution space is smooth, making it a good point of comparison to the Navier-Stokes dataset. Figure~\ref{fig:loss_vs_resolution_darcy_flow}
shows the MSE loss as a function of evaluation resolution for each
model, with the training resolution indicated by the vertical dashed
line. Table~\ref{tab:d-values-darcy-flow} reports the corresponding
discretization robustness score $D$.

Compared with the Navier-Stokes experiment, the four models are closer
in overall performance on the Darcy flow dataset. At the training
resolution, CNO\_standard achieves the lowest loss, followed by
FNO\_k16, CNO\_no\_UND, and FNO\_k8. The two FNO variants show a similar
pattern to that observed for Navier-Stokes; the loss is lowest near the
training resolution and increases when the model is evaluated at other
resolutions. This increase is stronger below the training resolution
than above it. Notably, FNO\_k8 outperforms FNO\_k16 at resolutions below $N = 88$. This indicates that
retaining more Fourier modes does not consistently improve performance
under transfer to coarser grids.

The CNO variants show much flatter loss curves across the evaluated
resolutions, with $D$ values of order \(10^{-2}\). At lower resolutions,
both CNO models maintain an almost constant loss, while at higher
resolutions both losses increase slowly. In contrast to the
Navier-Stokes experiment, CNO\_standard outperforms CNO\_no\_UND at all
evaluated resolutions. The loss of CNO\_no\_UND also increases more
quickly than that of CNO\_standard when evaluating above the training
resolution. Overall, the Darcy flow results show a clearer separation
between the FNO and CNO variants in terms of discretization robustness;
the FNO models are more sensitive to resolution transfer, while the CNO
models remain nearly constant across the tested range.

\subsection{FNO}

The FNOs perform poorly in terms of discretization invariance on both datasets. On Navier-Stokes, FNO_k8 has a comparable D-value to the CNOs, albeit with a significantly higher loss on all tested resolutions. This poor discretization invariance is due to the meaning of each mode being dependent on the spatial resolution. The FNO is only ever exposed to a single resolution during training, and thus learns an equivalence between a mode's index and its physical meaning. however, this does not necessarily hold under resolution transfers. when moving to higher resolutions, more modes are resolvable, which changes the energy distribution among modes. at resolutions different from testing, this implicit equivalence between physical meaning and mode index falls apart, leading to poor performance. \subsection{CNO}

Discuss the role of locality, the bandlimited assumption, and the
implicit multiscale structure in producing steadier behavior across
resolutions.

As the CNO always resamples onto the same internal grid, the model is very stable. on darcy flow, the loss remains almost constant at lower resolutions; this is because the solution operator and solution space for this dataset is very smooth, and thus the tested lower resolutions still capture sufficient detail. at higher resolutions, we see that the loss on both datasets increases almost linearly. this is because the CNO always bandlimits the input. the rate at which the loss increases when testing at higher resolutions is therefore proportional to the rate at which the bandlimited approximation diverges from the "true" field. 

We observe that the inclusion of the UND block improves performance for the CNO at all tested resolutions except high resolutions on the Navier-Stokes dataset specifically. This can be understood by considering what exactly the UND block does. As described in section \ref{cno architecture section}, the goal is to make the model more discretization invariant by not letting higher frequency content created by the nonlinearity fold into the retained modes. however, as mentioned in the theory section, the low pass filter applied during the downsampling back to the original resolution is not ideal. The utilized low pass filter effectively works by smoothing dataset, which works to erase high frequency content. While this works well to remove unresolved modes, it also affects and smooths the highest retained modes. This means that the highest retained modes are no longer available to the model to the same extent they would be without the smoothing. In other words, the inclusion of the UND block effectively applies an even stricter bandlimit to the input representation than the one enforced by the initial resampling. For the Darcy flow dataset, this has little consequence; the operator is heavily smoothing, so low frequency content dominates the solution space. Diminished access to the highest modes is therefore not detrimental. This is, however, not the case for the highly non-linear Navier-Stokes. The loss increase for CNO when tested at higher resolution is approximately linear on Navier-Stokes, and as already mentioned, the rate of loss increase is tied to the rate at which the training representation diverges from the higher bandwidth representations. as the CNO_no_UND effectively retains access to more modes, it stands to reason that it diverges from higher bandwidth representations slower. Despite this, the inclusion of the UND block is encouraged as it improves performance in most cases and is mathematically sound.

\subsection{improving the FNO}

FNO is the standard FNO; the other models have been modified to include resampling onto a fixed internal grid and/or use the UND block used by the CNO.         \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The FNO was modified to include parts of the CNO deemed responsible for its superior discretization invariance. These parts were found to be the UND block and the resampling onto a fixed grid. Four FNOs were examined; a standard FNO, an FNO with UND, an FNO with resampling, and an FNO with both UND and resampling. The models all retained 16 modes in each direction and were trained and tested on the Navier-Stokes dataset. The loss under resolution change was then tested with the results seen in figure \ref{fig:improving-fno} and the corresponding D-values in table \ref{tab:d-values-navier-stokes-improved-fno}. 

The UND block had a neglible, but positive, impact. The D-values are slightly lower for the FNOs with UND when compared to the equivalent FNO without. This shows aliasing is an issue, but that it is not the primary failure mechanism causing the poor discretization invariance. Much more notably, the FNOs with resampling show a dramatic improvement in discretization robustness.  The D-values for the FNOs with resampling are an order of magnitude lower than those without. This is because it fixes the problem outlined in section \ref{fno subsection}: the equivalence between mode index and physical meaning. Note again that the model performs artificially well on this dataset. As the initial condition is bandlimited to 16 modes, all resolutions tested here are fine enough to capture the entire signal. For datasets where this is not the case, we expect a gradual increase in loss when testing at higher resolutions. The rate at which the loss increases is proportional to the rate at which the bandlimited field diverges from its representations with higher bandlimits, as was found for the CNO. By resampling onto a fixed internal grid, this equivalence is restored, even when testing the model at different resolutions. By implementing parts of the CNO architecture, the FNO's performance under resolution transfer is dramatically improved.
