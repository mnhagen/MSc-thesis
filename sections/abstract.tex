Partial differential equations (PDEs) are central to scientific computing and engineering, but high-fidelity numerical simulations are often computationally expensive. Neural operators have recently emerged as a promising approach for surrogate modeling by learning mappings between function spaces rather than fixed-dimensional vectors.

A key challenge for such models is discretization invariance: the ability to generalize to spatial resolutions unseen during training. This thesis considers this property for Fourier- and convolution-based neural operators on uniform Cartesian grids, and for geometry-aware neural operators on non-uniform point cloud domains.

On uniform grids, the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) and the Convolutional Neural Operator (CNO) are evaluated on Navier--Stokes and Darcy flow benchmarks through resolution-transfer experiments. The results show that CNO exhibits substantially stronger robustness across resolutions, while FNO is more sensitive to discretization mismatch due to its reliance on a fixed set of Fourier modes. An ablation study shows that this sensitivity is primarily caused by the dependence on input resolution. Introducing resampling onto a fixed internal grid reduces this sensitivity by nearly two orders of magnitude.

For non-uniform meshes, the geometry-aware Fourier Neural Operator (Geo-FNO) is evaluated on incompressible flow past a cylinder and on a high-temperature gas-cooled reactor (HTGR) temperature dataset. The results show that Geo-FNO produces accurate predictions on irregular point clouds, while also highlighting that discretization invariance in the mesh-free setting must be interpreted as robustness to changes in sampling geometry rather than resolution transfer.
